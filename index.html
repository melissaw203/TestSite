About Figure Skating

Figure skating is becoming more and more popular, and is incredibly fun as a hobby or as a competitive sport. The four Olympic disciplines are men's singles, ladies' singles, pair skating, and ice dancing. Non-Olympic disciplines include synchronized skating and solo ice dance. Testing

Every figure skater knows about the skating level tests that are required for those who are serious about the sport. Figure skaters are tested on 4 main categories, moves in the field, pattern dance, free skate, and free dance. There are many rinks close to Greenwich that offer testing sessions for the different categories. Local rinks around Greenwich that offer these tests include Stamford Twin Rinks, Terry Conners, Rye Playland Ice Rink, and Evarts Rink. From experience, it is easier to pass and STR and Terry Conners and significantly harder to pass at Evarts Rink in Katonah.

Moves in the Field

The United States Figure Skating Club created 8 different moves in the field tests, each designed to test a skater's skills at a certain level and depthness. The levels, from easiest to hardest, include Pre-Preliminary, Preliminary, Pre-Juvenile, Juvenile, Intermediate, Novice, Junior, and Senior.

Pattern Dance & Solo Pattern Dance

The USFSC levels of ice dance tests are organized into 8 levels, each level consisting of 3 to 4 ice dances that must be learned and passed. These ice dances test technique, timing, and expression. The levels are Preliminary, Pre-Bronze, Bronze, Pre-Silver, Silver, Pre-Gold, Gold, and International.

Free Skate

The eight levels of free skate include Pre-Preliminary, Preliminary, Pre-Juvenile, Juvenile, Intermediate, Novice, Junior, and Senior. Free skates consist of a program to music that have certain requirements that must be fulfilled, such as jumps, spins, or certain turns in footwork. As the levels progress, so does the number of requirements and the length of the program.

Free Dance

Free dance tests are required for skaters to compete in most official figure skating competitions. They can be taken either as an individual or with a partner. In order to compete in a certain level at a competition, the skater has to have taken the corresponding free dance test. The five levels of free dance tests are juvenile, intermediate, novice, junior, and senior.

Synchronized Skating

There are several synchronized skating teams that are accessible from Greenwich. The biggest and most successful team is the Skyliners. With 12 separate levels, Skyliners is one of the biggest parent-run organizations. Read about the main qualifying levels below!

Juvenile

Coached by Natalie Martello, the Juvenile line of the Skyliners Synchronized Skating Team is definitely a force to be reckoned with. The defending national champions in the season of 2015 to 2016, these girls are part of a team that requires teamwork, sportsmanship, and skill. Check out a video of the Skyliners Junior line below!

Novice

The Novice division of the Skyliners requires much hard work and dedication. A huge step up from Juvenile, Novice competitors have more required elements and different rules. Silver medalists at the Eastern Sectional Championships, this past season's Novice team was incredible.

Junior

Surely the most difficult team to get onto, Skyliner's Junior team proudly represents the United States. Dubbed Team USA after defending their championship at Nationals, Junior travels to multiple out-of-country competitions a month.

Senior

Skyliners Senior also represents the United States in international competitions at the senior division. Earning a silver medal at Nationals, Senior is the ultimate dedication to the sport of synchronized figure skating.
